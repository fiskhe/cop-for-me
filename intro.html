Introduction and Key Terms

Anthropogenic Climate Change

Anthropogenic climate change refers to rising global temperature averages that are brought on by human activities, such as the release of increasing quantities of greenhouse gases (GHG) and particulate matter into the atmosphere. Climate change is more than just pollution, although both issues stem from human industrial activities and have serious consequences on human and environmental health. The long-term effects of global warming include more frequent and intensified natural disasters, including hurricanes; sea level rise that renders coastal areas uninhabitable; impacts on agricultural yields, which may contribute to global food insecurity; and devastating losses of biodiversity as species go extinct due to habitat loss. For more information on how we know that recent temperature trends are attributable to human actions, see the Visualizing Climate Scenarios page of this site.

The Role of the United Nations

The IPCC

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) is a scientific body of the United Nations founded in 1988 with the purpose of producing regular climate assessment reports. These reports supply policymakers with the scientific knowledge necessary to make informed decisions surrounding the mitigation of anthropogenic climate change. Thousands of experts work together, on a voluntary basis, to review relevant published literature and synthesize international climate findings in the report. The most recent Sixth Assessment Report (AR6) has released one of its three sections in 2021, and the entire report will be finalized by the end of 2022. A major finding of the IPCC is the need to limit the increase in the global temperature average to 1.5 °C in order to avoid catastrophic consequences.

UNFCCC and COP

Climate change is an inherently global phenomenon, so it makes sense that the United Nations is working to address this issue. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) was created in 1992 and came into effect in 1994. As its name suggests, the UNFCCC sets up a framework for countries to discuss climate change at an annual meeting called the Conference of the Parties (COP) . At these meetings, states usually aim to create or amend international climate treaties and agreements. The Kyoto Protocol and the Paris Agreement are two climate treaties that have resulted from COP meetings, and each is named after the host city of their respective COP.

The Paris Agreement

At the 21st Conference of the Parties (COP21) in 2015, the Paris Agreement was adopted as the most recent climate treaty among the Parties to the UNFCCC. The Paris Agreement replaced the first GHG reduction treaty, the Kyoto Protocol, that had held force since COP3 in 1997. The Kyoto Protocol was deemed an insufficient mechanism for reducing GHG emissions because three key parties never ratified it: the U.S., China. and India. In order to convince all Parties to join the Paris Agreement, some of the teeth of the previous treaty were removed. Under the “non-adversarial and non-punitive” Paris Agreement, countries set their own Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs), yet there are no penalties for failing to meet one’s NDC. Thus, a major criticism of the Paris Agreement is the lack of incentives for countries to set and meet ambitious reduction goals.

COP26

After COVID-19 caused the Parties not to meet in 2020, COP26 was held in Glasgow, UK from October 31st through November 12th, 2021. Prior to this meeting, the majority of Parties reestablished or updated their NDCs, as they are to do every five years beginning in 2020, and a new NDC Synthesis Report was released by the UN on October 25th, 2021. For more information on climate projections following COP26’s updates, see the Visualizing Climate Scenarios page of this site.

Climate Justice

Climate change is an environmental issue with international consequences. Spillover effects occur because all of the world’s countries share one global atmosphere. Regardless of where GHGs are emitted, the atmosphere can only handle a certain quantity of emissions before the average global temperature increases. While large, developed countries like the United States have contributed the most to GHG pollution, developing states and small island nations are already becoming the first countries to experience the damaging effects of climate change. The concept of climate justice highlights the ethical implications of climate change and the need for a just transition that allows poorer countries to develop sustainably, which can be achieved through technology sharing and proposed "loss and damage" funding.
